April 13, 2016 2016

During the past few weeks, I've been working on optimizing this site for mobile viewing. One major obstacle to mobile viewing seems to be the sticky nav. When 'positioned absolutely' the nav does not render its width to 100% of the viewport as indicated by its css. If a user presses on one of the four main navigational buttons the nav -- which becomes 'fixed' after a certain waypoint -- does expand its width to 100% of the viewport horizon. Sometimes, however, especially if the user opts to scroll past the waypoint instead, the nav disappears and reappears. This behavior is unpredictable. Part of my work during this time has been testing out edge cases to see what makes the nav behave this way. Ultimately, what I might do is to simply leave a hamburger icon on a non-moving nav; that is, I'm willing to sacrifice visual effects if this affords me a consistent, glitch-free and simple mobile experience.

April 14, 2016 2016

After telling my mentor of my attempts to render this page responsive to mobile (especially with regards to the sticky nav and the nav buttons), he pointed me in the direction of several flexgrid frameworks. Now these frameworks, like flexible grid or flexbox grid require the inclusion and hence utilization of bootstrap. Including bootstrap into this page will definitely solve some problems but it will also present me with other choices that I hadn't had to worry about previously: should I just bootstrap wholesale and lose all idiosyncrasy? Or should I spend more time learning how to retain the uniqueness of the website within the bootstrap framework?
